# Why most 5S programs die in month two

I've seen the same pattern in three different plants: leadership gets excited, red tags go up, everyone paints lines on the floor — and by week eight the tags are peeling and nobody remembers what "shine" was supposed to mean.

At my current site — a spiral welded steel pipe plant with 330 workers across three shifts — we ran into the same wall. What follows is what actually pulled us through.

## The three things that made it stick

**1. Foremen own the audit, not managers.**
When engineers audit 5S, workers see it as inspection. When the shift foreman does it, it becomes part of the shift handover. We built a one-page audit that takes 4 minutes per zone.

**2. Photo before, photo after — every single time.**
Written observations get argued with. Photos don't. We keep a shared album per zone. On day 90 you can scroll back and see what changed.

**3. Kill "sort" as a one-time event.**
Sort is not a launch day. It's the first ten minutes of every Friday shift. If you treat it as an event, drift wins.

## What I'd do differently

If I were starting over, I would spend less time on training slides and more time on the first zone. One area, done properly, becomes the reference. Everyone else copies it. Trying to launch across the whole plant at once dilutes ownership.
